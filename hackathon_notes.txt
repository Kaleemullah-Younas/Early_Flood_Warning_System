THE CRISIS
----------
In 2022, catastrophic flooding submerged one-third of Pakistan,
killed over 1,700 people, displaced 32 million, and caused more
than $30 billion in economic damage.

In 2025, floods returned. Over 6.9 million people were affected,
more than 1,000 killed (275 of them children), 229,700 houses
destroyed, and 2,811 km of roads damaged.

Pakistan does not have a flooding problem. It has a WARNING problem.
In both disasters, affected communities received less than 24 hours
of advance warning — not enough time to evacuate safely.

THE CHALLENGE
-------------
Build a flood risk early warning system for Pakistani districts.

Your team must deliver THREE things:

1. AN ML MODEL

Input Features:
Use the following features from the training data — selection and engineering is part of the challenge:
- Rainfall: `precipitation`, `precip_3day_avg`, `precip_7day_avg`
- Soil: `soil_moisture`, `soil_3day_avg`
- Water extent: `water_area_km2`, `water_area_change`, `water_area_pct_change`
- Atmospheric: `temperature`, `humidity`, `pressure`, `evaporation`
- Temporal: `month`, `day_of_year`, `is_monsoon`
- Flood history: `ds_idx` (read the data dictionary before using)
- Terrain: `avg_elevation_m` from `district_elevation_reference.csv` — merge on district

IMPORTANT: Do not use `elevation`, `latitude`, or `longitude` from the training data — they are constant and carry no signal.

Output:
- Primary: flood risk classification mapped from model probability
     Low: < 25% | Medium: 25–50% | High: 50–75% | Critical: > 75%
- Secondary: confidence score displayed as a percentage (0–100%)
- Target column to predict: `flood_event` (0 = no flood, 1 = flood)

Minimum Requirements
- Model accuracy > 70% on a held-out test split
- Must handle `NaN` values in `precipitation` without crashing — document your imputation strategy
- Must handle `inf` values in `water_area_pct_change` — clip or replace before training
- Duplicate rows must be removed before train/test split
- Phantom rows with impossible values must be detected and excluded

What Strong Models Do
- Engineer lag features and rolling averages rather than using raw daily readings alone
- Explain feature importance — which inputs drove a High or Critical prediction and why
- Use ensemble methods (XGBoost, Random Forest) over single decision trees
- Account for class imbalance (32% flood vs 68% non-flood) explicitly

NOTE: A model with 78% accuracy whose team can explain every prediction is stronger than a 92% black box. Interpretability is scored.

2. A USER INTERFACE
Built with Streamlit, Gradio, or React. A non-technical district official must be able to use it with zero guidance.

Inputs
- Rainfall (mm), date, district, soil condition (Dry/Moist/Saturated), visible surface water (Yes/No)
- Labels must be plain English — no variable names or technical terms visible to the user

Output
- Large colour-coded risk badge: Green (Low) / Yellow (Medium) / Orange (High) / Red (Critical)
- Confidence score, estimated population at risk, and one specific recommended action per risk level
- All risk labels in both English and Urdu simultaneously

Uncertainty Handling
- Missing inputs must prompt the user — not crash
- Insufficient or out-of-range data must display *"Insufficient data — manual assessment recommended"* rather than forcing a classification

Performance
- Loads within 10 seconds on a slow connection
- No heavy libraries, map tiles, or external dependencies loaded at runtime
- Total page size under 2MB on first load

Hard Rules
- No ML jargon visible anywhere (no "model", "inference", "prediction", "feature")
- No login or setup steps required
- Must not break on missing or extreme input values

NOTE: Judges will test the UI live — including blank fields and out-of-range values. A plain interface that handles edge cases beats a polished one that crashes.

3. A 3-MINUTE IMPACT PITCH
   - Who benefits, and by how much?
   - How many hours of additional warning would your system have
     provided before the August 15, 2025 Buner disaster?
   - What happens when your model is wrong? (Defend your failure modes)
